Skewness and Data Distribution

Skewness describes the degree of asymmetry in a data distribution around its mean. While measures of central tendency and dispersion summarize data numerically, skewness explains the *shape* of the distribution.

Understanding skewness is essential in engineering, data science, machine learning, economics, and network performance analysis.

Data Distribution

A data distribution shows how values are spread across different ranges. It is commonly visualized using histograms or frequency curves.

  • Symmetric distribution: Mean = Median = Mode
  • Positively skewed distribution: Mean > Median > Mode
  • Negatively skewed distribution: Mean < Median < Mode

The shape of the distribution affects the choice of statistical methods and interpretation of results.

Skewness

Skewness measures the lack of symmetry in a frequency distribution. A perfectly symmetric distribution has zero skewness.

  • Zero Skewness: Symmetric distribution
  • Positive Skewness: Tail extends to the right
  • Negative Skewness: Tail extends to the left

Karl Pearson‚Äôs Coefficient of Skewness

Formula (when mode is known):

Sk = (Mean ‚àí Mode) / Standard Deviation

Formula (when mode is not known):

Sk = 3(Mean ‚àí Median) / Standard Deviation

Solved Example (Positive Skewness)

  • Mean = 60
  • Median = 55
  • Standard Deviation = 10

Calculation:

Sk = 3(60 ‚àí 55) / 10 = 15 / 10 = 1.5

Since skewness is positive, the distribution is right-skewed.

Bowley‚Äôs method is used when quartiles are available and is suitable for open-ended distributions.

  • Q‚ÇÅ = First quartile

  • Q‚ÇÉ = Third quartile

  • Q‚ÇÅ = 30
  • Q‚ÇÇ = 40

Sk = (45 + 30 ‚àí 2√ó40) / (45 ‚àí 30) = (75 ‚àí 80) / 15 = -0.33
