Addition and Multiplication Rules of Probability

In probability theory, the addition and multiplication rules are fundamental principles used to calculate the probability of combined events. These rules are widely applied in engineering, data science, communication systems, and risk analysis.

Addition Rule of Probability

The addition rule is used to find the probability that at least one of two events occurs.

1. For Mutually Exclusive Events

P(A ‚à™ B) = P(A) + P(B)

Example:

  • Experiment: Tossing a coin
  • Event A: Head ‚Üí P(A) = 1/2
  • Event B: Tail ‚Üí P(B) = 1/2
  • P(A ‚à™ B) = 1/2 + 1/2 = 1

Addition Rule for Non-Mutually Exclusive Events

When two events can occur simultaneously, their common probability must be subtracted to avoid double counting.

P(A ‚à™ B) = P(A) + P(B) ‚àí P(A ‚à© B)

Example:

  • From a deck of cards:
  • Event A: Card is a King ‚Üí P(A) = 4/52
  • Event B: Card is a Heart ‚Üí P(B) = 13/52
  • Event A ‚à© B: King of Hearts ‚Üí P = 1/52

P(A ‚à™ B) = (4 + 13 ‚àí 1) / 52 = 16 / 52

Multiplication Rule of Probability

The multiplication rule is used to find the probability that two events occur together.

1. For Independent Events

P(A ‚à© B) = P(A) √ó P(B)

Example:

  • Experiment: Tossing a coin and rolling a die
  • P(Head) = 1/2
  • P(Number 6) = 1/6

P(A ‚à© B) = (1/2) √ó (1/6) = 1/12

Multiplication Rule for Dependent Events

When events are dependent, the probability of the second event depends on the occurrence of the first event.

P(A ‚à© B) = P(A) √ó P(B | A)

Example:

  • Drawing two cards without replacement
  • Event A: First card is an Ace ‚Üí P(A) = 4/52
  • Event B: Second card is an Ace given first is Ace ‚Üí P(B|A) = 3/51

P(A ‚à© B) = (4/52) √ó (3/51) = 12 / 2652

The addition and multiplication rules form the backbone of probability theory. Correct identification of event relationships ensures accurate modeling of uncertain systems in engineering and data-driven applications.
